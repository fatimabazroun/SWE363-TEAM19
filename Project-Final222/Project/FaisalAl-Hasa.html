Growing Up in Aramco

Written by: Faisal Al-Hasa Published by: Hana Ahmad Genre: Memoir Region of Origin: ash Sharqiyah Word Count: 574

Childhood tales from the company camp are often filled with a sense of wonder, adventure, and a distinct feeling of belonging. Growing up in Aramco, Saudi Arabia’s oil company, was like being part of a unique community—a microcosm where cultures blended, traditions were honored, and life was shaped by the rhythms of the oil industry. For the children of Aramco employees, it was a childhood like no other, where the world outside the camp felt distant, and everything needed for a fulfilling life could be found within its borders. The company camp itself was a self-sustained, almost idyllic world. With neatly landscaped gardens, playgrounds, schools, and sports fields, it was a place that seemed to meet every need. Aramco built a community that felt safe and close-knit, a place where neighbors knew each other, and friendships often stretched across continents and cultures. Children were free to roam the wide streets, explore the sand dunes, or play sports in the many recreation areas, with little concern for the world beyond the camp. There was a unique sense of freedom that comes with growing up in a place where the boundaries between work and home, family and community, were beautifully blurred. For many children, the most exciting part of life in Aramco was the sheer diversity of experiences. The camp brought together people from all over the world—Americans, Arabs, Filipinos, Europeans, and many others—each with their own customs, stories, and traditions. This melting pot of cultures enriched the experience, exposing children to multiple languages, cuisines, and traditions from an early age. Learning new languages came easily; playground games often involved a mixture of English, Arabic, and other languages, making every interaction an opportunity for discovery. Aramco also made sure its residents had access to world-class facilities. The schools were excellent, often offering a curriculum that was more advanced than many other places around the world. The teachers were highly qualified, many of whom were expatriates brought in specifically to teach the children of Aramco employees. School events, including sports days, talent shows, and field trips, were central to childhood in the camp. These events fostered not only a sense of competition but also a strong community spirit, as parents, teachers, and children all came together to celebrate achievements and share experiences. Despite the advantages of living in such a well-supported environment, there was also a sense of isolation that came with living in a company town. The camp was physically separated from the bustling city life of Dhahran and the outside world, which meant that while residents had everything they needed within the camp, they were often cut off from the cultural life of Saudi Arabia itself. For children, this created a bubble-like environment—where the world felt like it began and ended within the gates of the camp. This didn’t feel confining, though. Instead, it provided an almost magical backdrop for childhood, where imagination could roam freely in the wide-open spaces of the desert and playgrounds. One of the most significant aspects of growing up in Aramco was the sense of camaraderie and shared identity. The families living in the camp were united by the common thread of their connection to the company. Employees worked in a variety of roles, from engineers and managers to scientists and laborers, all contributing to the growth of Aramco. Children grew up hearing stories of their parents’ work in the oil fields and refineries, learning from a young age the importance of the company in shaping not only their lives but the future of Saudi Arabia itself. Weekends and holidays in Aramco were often filled with picnics, barbecues, and desert adventures. The families would gather for events like the annual company picnic, where there were games, music, and performances, or they would drive out into the desert for a peaceful day in nature. The vast, open landscape of the desert, with its sweeping dunes and stunning sunsets, became a playground for the children. Whether it was a casual game of soccer on the sand or a desert safari with the family, these memories became treasured moments of their youth. Of course, there were challenges as well. The remoteness of the camp meant that once in a while, families had to deal with the difficulties of living far from their home countries. The long distances between the camp and other major cities meant that vacations were often planned well in advance, and visits from relatives were rare. Yet, the relationships built in the camp filled the gaps. Neighbors became like extended family, and friendships were deep and lasting. Growing up in Aramco meant living in a world that was both insulated and diverse, a place where childhood was rich with experiences, stories, and adventures. For many, those memories of endless desert vistas, close-knit communities, and international friendships would stay with them for a lifetime. Life in Aramco was more than just an ordinary childhood—it was a unique chapter in a broader, global story.
